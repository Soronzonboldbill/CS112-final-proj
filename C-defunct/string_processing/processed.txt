<i><b>Monty Python's The Meaning of Life</b></i>, also known simply as <i><b>The Meaning of Life</b></i>, is a 1983 British <a href="/wiki/Musical_film" title="Musical film">musical</a> <a href="/wiki/Sketch_comedy" title="Sketch comedy">sketch comedy</a> film written and performed by the <a href="/wiki/Monty_Python" title="Monty Python">Monty Python</a> troupe, directed by <a href="/wiki/Terry_Jones" title="Terry Jones">Terry Jones</a>. <i>The Meaning of Life</i> was the last feature film to star all six Python members before the death of <a href="/wiki/Graham_Chapman" title="Graham Chapman">Graham Chapman</a> in 1989.
<Six fish in a restaurant's <a href="/wiki/Fish_tank" class="mw-redirect" title="Fish tank">tank</a> greet each other, then see their friend being eaten. This leads them to question the <a href="/wiki/Meaning_of_life" title="Meaning of life">meaning of life</a>. In the first sketch, "The Miracle of Birth", maternity doctors ignore a woman in labour while trying to impress the hospital's administrator. In <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Catholic Church in the United Kingdom">Roman Catholic</a> man loses his job and informs his numerous children that he must sell them for scientific experiments ("<a href="/wiki/Every_Sperm_Is_Sacred" title="Every Sperm Is Sacred">Every Sperm Is Sacred</a>"). A <a href="/wiki/Protestantism_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Protestantism in the United Kingdom">Protestant</a> man looks on disapprovingly and proudly remarks that Protestants can use contraception and have sex for pleasure (although his wife observes that they never do).
<According to Palin, "the writing process was quite cumbersome. An awful lot of material didn't get used. <i>Holy Grail</i> had a structure, a loose one: the search for the grail. Same with <i>Life of Brian</i>. With this, it wasn't so clear. In the end, we just said: 'Well, what the heck. We have got lots of good material, let's give it the loosest structure, which will be the meaning of life'".<sup id="cite_ref-guard2013_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guard2013-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
<Principal photography began on 12 July 1982 and was completed about two months later, on 11 September. A wide variety of locations were used, such as <a href="/wiki/Porchester_Hall" title="Porchester Hall">Porchester Hall</a> in <a href="/wiki/Queensway,_London" title="Queensway, London">Queensway</a> for the Mr Creosote sketch, where hundreds of pounds of fake vomit had to be cleaned up on the last day due to a wedding being scheduled hours later. The Malham Moors were chosen for the Grim Reaper segment; the countryside near <a href="/wiki/Strathblane" title="Strathblane">Strathblane</a> was used for the Zulu War; and "Every Sperm Is Sacred" was shot in <a href="/wiki/Colne" title="Colne">Colne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>, with interiors done at <a href="/wiki/Elstree_Studios_(Shenley_Road)" title="Elstree Studios (Shenley Road)">Elstree Studios</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  The school chapel scene with the song "O Lord, Please Don't Burn Us" was shot at <a href="/wiki/Churchill_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Churchill College">Churchill College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>.
<The <a href="/wiki/Short_film" title="Short film">short film</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Crimson_Permanent_Assurance" title="The Crimson Permanent Assurance">The Crimson Permanent Assurance</a></i> introduces the feature. It is about a group of elderly office clerks working in a small <a href="/wiki/Accounting" title="Accounting">accounting</a> firm. They rebel against <a href="/wiki/Yuppie" title="Yuppie">yuppie</a> corporate masters, transform their office building into a <a href="/wiki/Pirate_ship" class="mw-redirect" title="Pirate ship">pirate ship</a>, and raid a large financial district. One of the boardrooms raided reappears later in the film, from shortly before the attack begins until the narrator apologises and a skyscraper falls and crushes the marauders.
<The original <a href="/wiki/Tagline" title="Tagline">tagline</a> read "It took God six days to create the Heavens and the Earth, and Monty Python just 90 minutes to screw it up"<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> (the length of <i>The Meaning of Life</i> proper is 90 minutes, but becomes 107 minutes as released with  the "Short Subject Presentation", <i>The Crimson Permanent Assurance</i>). In an April 2012 re-release held by the <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Institute" title="American Film Institute">American Film Institute</a>, the tagline is altered to read "It took God six days to create the Heavens and the Earth, and Monty Python just 1 hour and 48 minutes to screw it up".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
<The film opened in the United States on 31 March 1983. At 257 cinemas it ranked number six at the US box office, grossing US$1,987,853 ($7,734 per screen) in its opening weekend. It played at 554 cinemas at its widest point, and its total gross in the United States and Canada was $14,929,552.<sup id="cite_ref-mojo_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mojo-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the United Kingdom it opened on 3 screens in London and grossed £49,641 in its first seven days to rank third at the London box office.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Internationally it grossed $27.8 million for a worldwide total of $42.7 million.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
<<a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Sun-Times" title="Chicago Sun-Times">Chicago Sun-Times</a></i> gave the film two and a half stars out of four, calling it a "a barbed, uncompromising attack on generally observed community standards".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Vincent_Canby" title="Vincent Canby">Vincent Canby</a> declared it "the <i><a href="/wiki/Ben_Hur_(1959_film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ben Hur (1959 film)">Ben Hur</a></i> of sketch films, which is to say that it's a tiny bit out of proportion", concluding it was amusing, but he wished it were consistently amusing.<sup id="cite_ref-Canby_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canby-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> staff assessed it as disgusting, ridiculous, tactless, but above all, amusing.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gene_Siskel" title="Gene Siskel">Gene Siskel</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> awarded 3 stars out of 4, calling it "fresh and original and delightfully offensive. What more can you ask of a comedy?"<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Sheila Benson of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> wrote that the film was full of "raunchy talk, blasphemy (well, sacrilege) and one example of what kids call a totally <i>gnarly,</i> gross-out scene. The problem for the reviewer (to be specific, this reviewer) is when you are laughing this much it makes logging all the fast-flying offenses almost impossible."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Gary Arnold of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> was negative, writing that "The strongest impressions left by this picture have less to do with its largely tedious attempts to burlesque human weakness and pomposity than with the group's failure to evolve a coherent satiric outlook."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A review by Steve Jenkins in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Monthly_Film_Bulletin" title="The Monthly Film Bulletin">The Monthly Film Bulletin</a></i> was also negative, writing that the return to a sketch format constituted a "great leap backwards" for the troupe and that the film's outrageous moments "cannot disguise the overall air of <i>déjà vu</i> and playing it safe."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
<<i>The Meaning of Life</i> was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Prix_(Cannes_Film_Festival)" title="Grand Prix (Cannes Film Festival)">Grand Jury Prize</a> at the <a href="/wiki/1983_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="1983 Cannes Film Festival">1983 Cannes Film Festival</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-festival-cannes.com_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-festival-cannes.com-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> While the Cannes jury, led by <a href="/wiki/William_Styron" title="William Styron">William Styron</a>, were fiercely split on their opinions on several films in competition, <i>The Meaning of Life</i> had general support, securing it the second-highest honour after the <a href="/wiki/Palme_d%27Or" title="Palme d&#39;Or">Palme d'Or</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ballad_of_Narayama_(1983_film)" title="The Ballad of Narayama (1983 film)">The Ballad of Narayama</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
<A two-disc <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> release in 2003 features a documentary on production and a <a href="/wiki/Director%27s_cut" title="Director&#39;s cut">director's cut</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> which adds deleted scenes into the film, making it 116 minutes. The first is <i>The Adventures of Martin Luther</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> inserted after the scene with the Protestant couple talking about condoms. The second is a promotional video about the British army, which comes between the marching around the square scene and the Zulu army scene. The third and last is an extension of the American characters performed by Idle and Palin; they are shown their room and talk about <a href="/wiki/Tampon" title="Tampon">tampons</a>. In <a href="/wiki/DVD_region_code#1" title="DVD region code">Region 1</a>, it was released on <a href="/wiki/Blu-ray" title="Blu-ray">Blu-ray</a> to mark its 30th anniversary.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
In May 2020, it was released on <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a> in the United Kingdom.
